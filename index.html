Understanding the census geographies used in rural definitions

States in a nation

States are the primary governmental divisions of the United States and are the first level below nation in the United States Census Bureau's hierarchy of geography. States are too broad of a geography to be used to define rurality.

Counties in a state

Counties are the primary legal divisions of most states and are a popular geographical unit for defining rurality. Federal definitions and agencies which use counties to define rurality include:

  • Core-based Statistical Areas
  • Rural-Urban Continuum Codes
  • Urban Influence Codes
  • Center for Disease Control
Counties typically underestimate the rural population because rural pockets of metropolitan counties are classified as urban.

Tracts in a county

Census tracts are small, relatively permanent statistical subdivisions of a county or equivalent entity. The primary purpose of census tracts is to provide a stable set of geographic units for the presentation of statistical data. Census tracts generally have a population size between 1,200 and 8,000 people, with an optimum size of 4,000 people. A census tract usually covers a contiguous area; however, the spatial size of census tracts varies widely depending on the density of settlement.

Federal definitions and agencies which use counties to define rurality include:

  • Federal Housing Finance Agency
  • Federal Office of Rural Health Policy
  • Rural-Urban Commuting Area Codes
Because tracts are more specific than counties, tract-level rural definitions provide more nuanced estimates of rurality.

Select definitions with care

When selecting a definition to use, keep in mind the geography of that definition. Depending on whether the definition uses county, tract, zip, or block, it may be over-counting or under-counting certain rural populations.
